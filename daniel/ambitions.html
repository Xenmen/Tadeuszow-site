"What's the biggest problem in your field, and why aren't you working on it?"

(paraphrase of Nina Simon, in reference to Richard Hamming, Aaron Swartz also had some thoughts on this )


I've adopted computer science as my field, and there are certainly a lot of large problems to solve. Most pressing are UI/UX inconsistencies that make the lives of developers and users alike a nightmare, legal/technical restrictions on citizens' rights to create, modify, and share information, and compromised hardware that puts the lives and livelihoods of everyone at risk.

The 1st issue is steadily being addressed by small groups of clever, artistically-minded people around the world [Pitivi] [ElementaryOS] [ArkOS]. The 2nd and 3rd issues are a combination of legal and technical challenges; the FSF and EFF are doing excellent work already on the legal matters, though they are strapped for funding. There are some people working on affordable circuit-manufacturing tools, though they are very few in number and have even less funding available.

There's a common theme here, namely that the biggest obvious problems are already being solved. However, the people solving those problems lack resources. On reflection, I realized I had less value to offer in directly working with these noble groups on solving problems than I did in recognizing and addressing their resource problems.

Food (and water), housing, and access to computing facilities are the primary needs of these groups. I want to collect all the research already done on the topics of automated agriculture, affordable housing solutions, and homebrew computers, since these are all closely related issues for the demographic I want to serve (the demographic that is working hardest to serve the rest of the world).
